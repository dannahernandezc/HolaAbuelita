# Warning signs in mood and language — reference guide

## Purpose
This guide helps classify an older adult's responses about how they feel, based on the language they use. It is a support guide for prioritizing when family members should step in, and does not replace a professional medical or psychological evaluation.

## Normal level
Responses that express ordinary well-being, even with minor, one-off complaints typical of daily life:
- "Fine, as usual"
- "A bit tired but okay"
- "Calm, watching my show"
- Mentions of everyday activities (cooking, walking, talking with neighbors)

## Moderate attention level
Language suggesting mild discomfort, occasional loneliness, or a minor symptom worth keeping an eye on:
- "A bit sad today"
- "My back or knee hurts a little, nothing serious"
- "I haven't been sleeping well lately"
- "It's been a boring day, nobody has come by"
- The same minor complaint repeated for 2 or 3 days in a row (the repetition is the signal, not the complaint itself)

## Urgent level
Language suggesting intense pain, confusion, a fall, severe isolation, or hopelessness:
- Mentions of falls or being hurt ("I fell", "I slipped")
- Pain described as strong, intense, or not going away
- Confusion about the day, time, or familiar people
- Hopeless phrases ("I don't want to keep going like this", "what's the point of going on")
- No response to a routine check-in, with no prior explanation

## Note for the agent
If a response combines a warning word (for example "fall" or "strong pain") with a reassuring tone ("but I'm fine now"), prioritize the warning word. It is better to over-classify as moderate attention than to miss something: the cost of an extra check is low, the cost of missing something is not.
