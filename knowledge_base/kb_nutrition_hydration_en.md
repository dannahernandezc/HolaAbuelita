# Nutrition and hydration in older adults — reference guide

## Purpose
This guide helps classify responses related to meals and fluid intake. It does not replace a professional nutritional assessment.

## Why it matters
Older adults who spend part of the day alone tend to eat less than they need, especially when cooking takes effort or when they eat "out of habit" without real hunger. Mild dehydration is also common and can cause confusion, dizziness, and weakness — symptoms that are sometimes mistaken for normal aging.

## Normal level
- "Yes, already had breakfast" or "already had lunch"
- Mentions a recognizable meal (soup, rice, fruit, bread, etc.)
- "Yes, I've been drinking water" or mentions fluids naturally

## Moderate attention level
- "I ate a little, wasn't hungry"
- "Just had coffee, haven't eaten yet" (if it's already late in the day)
- No mention of fluids at all for one or two days
- "Didn't feel like cooking"

## Urgent level
- "I haven't eaten anything" combined with another symptom (dizziness, weakness, confusion)
- Several days in a row with no meals reported
- Mentions of dizziness, intense dry mouth, or weakness when standing up — possible signs of dehydration

## Note for the agent
An isolated, one-off lack of appetite is normal for anyone. Repeated missed meals, or their combination with dizziness or weakness, is the pattern that should escalate the alert level.
